What is Epidemiology?

the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related states or events in specified populations, and the application to the control of health problems

- A Dictionary of Epidemiology, 2008

underlying premise: health status affected by genetic, environmental, behavourial, social factors

Objectives of Epidemiology

  1. identify disease etiology (cause) and risk factors
  2. determine extent of disease
  3. study the disease's natural history and prognosis
  4. evaluate existing and new preventative and therapeutic measures and health care delivery
  5. develop public policy

1) identify disease etiology (cause) and risk factors (factors increasing a person's risk of disease)

  • how transmitted, why arises from risk behaviours?
  • aim: intervene to reduce morbidity and mortality -> develop prevention programs by identifying and reduce/eliminate exposure to causal factors, develop vaccines and treatments to prevent transmission

    • 2) determine extent of disease

  • what is the burden of disease in the community?
  • aim: plan health services/facilities, estimate how future health care providers should be trained

    • 3) study the disease's natural history and prognosis

  • severity, duration of survival
  • aim - quantitatively define baseline natural history of disease, compare baseline data with results from using newly developled intervention to test if new approaches are effective

    • 4) evaluate existing and new preventative and therapeutic measures and health care delivery

  • does cancer screening test improve survival of cancer patients? has growth of health care system impacted patients' health outcomes or quality of life?
  • aim: if yes, what is nature, how measured?

    • 5) developing public policy

  • environmental, genetic, social, behavioural considerations of disease prevention and health promotion
  • is radiation from kitchen microwaves a health hazard, what regulations required to reduce risks?

    • Changing Patterns

    Epidemiology provides clues to changes in health problems in communities over time

    e.g. 1 change in diseases affecting U.S. from 1900 to 2014:

    In 1900, infectious diseases (pneumonia and influenza) leading cause of death

    Developing countries became industrialised, leading cause of death similar to developed countries: chronic diseases (heart disease, cancer)

    Rise in infectious diseases in developed countries: human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) and tuberculosis increasing

    e.g. 2 change in U.S. life expectancy from 1900, 1950, 2014 at birth and age 65:

    expectancy increased at birth, due to less child mortality, but not much change in older age, suggest more needs to be done to extend lifespan

    Epidemiology and Prevention

    Epidemiological evidence used to:

    1. identify high-risk subgroups

      2. opportunity for early disease detection in new untested populations

    2. identify high-risk factors:
      • non-modifiable: age, sex, ethnicity
      • modifiable: obesity, smoking, diet, sexual practices, lifestyle factors

      opportunity to develop and introduce preventation to change exposures/risk factors

    Epidemiology and Clinical Practice

    clinical practice is population-based

    • diagnoses
      • based on correlating clinical findings with knowledge from community or autopsy/test/patient study results
    • prognoses
      • based on experience of large patient groups with same disease and treatment
    • therapy selection
      • based on randomized clinical trials studying effect of treatments on large patient groups

    The Epidemiologic Approach

    How does an epidemiologist identify disease cause?

    1. determine if association exists between exposure to a characteristic and presence of disease
      • study characteristics of groups and individuals
    2. identify from any associations if there is a possible causal relationship (not all associations are causal)

    investigate from descriptive data - ensure data is valid before interpreting it

    are these differences real? why are there differences?

    advert for community fluoride by Harrison County Board of Health

    example: effect of fluoride on tooth decay in two New York communities (1950s)

  • similar rates of tooth decay, one community added fluoride to water, after 10 years tooth decay rate much less

    • further example: demonstrate causal relationship between fluoride and low tooth decay

  • in other communities - removing fluoride increased dental caries again

    • From Observations to Preventative Actions

    3 case studies from history

    Ignaz Semmelweis and Childbed Fever

    Ignaz Philipp Semmelweis, Hungarian German Physician. In charge of the First Obstetrical Clinic of the General Hospital in Vienna, July 1846. Puerperal (childbed) fever was common.

    Pregnant women admitted for childbirth to either the:

  • First clinic

    • staffed by physcians and med students - performed autopsies in the morning on women who'd died from childbed fever.

  • Second clinic

    • staffed by midwives - didn't perform autopsies.

    Mortality in First Clinic (16%) more than twice as much in the Second Clinic (7%)

    Semmelweis observed First Clinic mortality was higher because physicians / students went directly from autopsies to deliveries

    Semmelweis suggested physician and student hands were transmitting 'cadaverous' particles from cadavers to birthing women

  • Germ Theory of disease wasn't established at this time

    • Suspicions confirmed when friend and colleague Jakob Kolletschka died from infection from accidental puncture of student's knife during an autopsy. Kolletschka's autospy similar pathology to women with childbed fever.

    Semmelweis then developed a policy :

    First Clinic staff to wash hands and brush under fingernails after autopsies and before seeing patients.

    In 1848, mortality in the First Clinic dropped from 12.2% to 2.4%, similar to the Second Clinic.

    Semmelweis was later replaced by a physican who removed the handwashing policy - First Clinic mortality rates rose - further evidence supporting causal relationship.

    It took time for Semmelweis' ideas to be accepted by the scientific community and for handwashing to be broadly adopted

    Lesson: successful policy making requires clearly presenting evidence, feasible and cost-effective intervention, and professional, public and political support

    Edward Jenner and Smallpox

    Edward Jenner, born in 1749, interested in smallpox - a worldwide scourge, killed 400K people a year and blinded 1/3 of survivors.

    It was known that survivors were immune to the disease

    Preventative measures: it became practice to infect healthy individuals with material from smallpox patients.

    Problems: some of those infected died from smallpox, infected others, developed other infections

    Dairy maids known to develop cowpox, a mild disease. During smallpox outbreaks, dairy maids seemed not to develop smallpox.

    Jenner heard a maid say it was because she already had the cowpox.

    Based on observational data alone, Jenner tested his hypothesis:

  • took cowpox material from dairy maid, Sarah Nelmes, and gave it to 8y/o James Phipps
  • 6 weeks later, gave the child smallpox material
  • James didn't get smallpox

    • Jenner had created a better preventative intervention

    In 1967, the World Health Organization (WHO) began international efforts to prevent smallpox using the cowpox-based vaccines.

    In 1980, WHO declared smallpox globally eradicated

    John Snow and Cholera

    John Snow, anesthesiologist in the 19th century, when cholera was a major problem in England.

    In 1854, 600 people living near the Broad Street pump in London died of cholera

    Snow believed cholera was transmitted by contaminated water (which went against the commonly held theory of disease at the time)

    Water companies used polluted Thames River water. When one company, the Lambeth Company, moved to a less polluted area, Snow thought mortality rate would decrease.

    Snow counted cholera deaths per households and determined the water supplier.

  • Found lower death rates in Lambeth-supplied areas
  • Found many victims drank from the Broad Street pump

    • Evidence led to intervention (temporary removal of pump handle), led to prevention of some cases

    Snow's conclusion that contaminated water was associated with cholera - based on observational data

    Lesson: it's not always necessary to understand the biology to prevent diseease

    e.g. smoking and lung cancer

  • unknown: specific agents in cigarettes cause cancer
  • known: more than 80% of lung cancer cases caused by smoking

    • NB/ both research and developing observation-based public health programs are important

    When Disease Frequency Declines, Who Deserves the Credit?

    Mortality rates from common infectious diseases decreased in the US in the last 100 years

    Tempting to link declines to improved treatments or vaccines made available in this time.

    However, Edward Kass' graphs (1971) and McKinlays' graphs (1977) show major decline in mortality occurred years before effective treatment available.

    How to explain this?

    Theoretically - human exposure to organisms involved may have declined, or harmfulness of organism diminished.

    Primarily result from improvements in social conditions, safer waters, reduced exposures to pollutants, improved nutrition and santitation - unrelated to medical intervention.

    Lesson - be cautious before attributing medical intervention to declined mortality over time.

    This is why epidemiologic studies are essential to assess medical intervention effectiveness, difficult to infer from only population-wide mortality

    Integrating Prevention and Treatment

    Often seen as mutually exclusive, preventation is integral to treatment in public health and clinical practice.

    Treatment of disease includes prevention: of death, complications, impact on people. Therapy involves secondary and tertiary prevention mostly, e.g. disability

    Nowadays, drugs used for patients with clear diagnoses of the disease are now being evaluated for use as drugs that can prevent the disease in high-risk patients

    e.g. studies: HIV and Alzheimer disease

    Conclusion

    Epidemiology is a tool that provides a rational basis for planning and implementing effective prevention

    used in clinical investigations: evaluate new and used therapies and new interventions for disease prevention

    ultimate goal: use prevention and treatment to improve control of disease, which will prevent deaths amd improve quality of life
